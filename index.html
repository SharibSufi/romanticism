The Rise of Romanticism

A literary journey through emotion, imagination, and beauty

Before Romanticism officially began, the Enlightenment dominated the literary world with its focus on logic, science, and reason. But during this time, some writers and artists started to rebel against this cold, structured thinking. They felt that human emotions, spirituality, and nature were being ignored. This quiet rebellion laid the foundation for a powerful literary movement that would soon take center stage.

The early phase of Romanticism emerged as a response to the need for deeper emotional expression. Writers began to focus on themes like childhood innocence, personal freedom, and the beauty of the natural world. During this period, William Blake stood out with his powerful collection Songs of Innocence and Experience. Meanwhile, Goethe’s The Sorrows of Young Werther stirred deep emotions across Europe.

This period gave rise to Wordsworth, Coleridge, Byron, Shelley, and Mary Shelley. Lyrical Ballads was a landmark collection and Frankenstein became an icon of Romantic literature—full of emotion, imagination, and philosophical questions about life and humanity.

Romanticism began to lean into mystery, death, and the supernatural. Edgar Allan Poe’s haunting works and Emily Brontë’s Wuthering Heights revealed the rawest parts of the human experience—love, madness, grief, and obsession.

Romanticism’s spirit lives on in modern poetry, music, and storytelling. It showed the world how powerful emotion and imagination can be. From Gothic to modern creative works, Romanticism made us feel something deeper.

Summary of Romanticism

Romanticism was a movement of passion, imagination, and rebellion against the coldness of logic. It was about embracing nature, dreaming wildly, and feeling deeply. The writers and poets of this time gave us emotional powerhouses—stories of love, loss, longing, and the mystical forces of the world. It was a turning point in literature where heart finally took center stage over the mind.

Why I Chose Romanticism

I chose Romanticism because it speaks to the emotional, creative part of me. It’s raw, beautiful, and real. I love how the authors poured their hearts into their words and how nature wasn’t just scenery—it was a character. This movement feels like a warm, expressive space in history, and I felt really inspired making this project. It made me fall in love with literature all over again.
